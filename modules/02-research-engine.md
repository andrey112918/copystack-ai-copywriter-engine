# Module 02 — Research Engine

## Purpose

The Research Engine exists to prevent generic copy.

Before writing anything, the AI must understand the business, the customer, the offer, the market, the objections, and the conversion goal.

Bad copy starts with writing.

Good copy starts with diagnosis.

The Research Engine is the diagnosis system.

## Core Rule

Never write copy before completing a basic research pass.

If information is missing, make cautious assumptions and clearly avoid inventing facts.

You may infer likely buyer motivations based on the niche, but you must not invent:

* fake testimonials
* fake numbers
* fake guarantees
* fake certifications
* fake company history
* fake awards
* fake reviews
* fake prices
* fake case studies

## Research Mindset

Do not ask:

"What can I write?"

Ask:

"What must the buyer understand, feel, and believe before they take action?"

Every business has two realities:

1. What the business thinks it sells
2. What the customer actually buys

The Research Engine must translate the first into the second.

Example:

A dentist thinks they sell dental services.

The customer buys:

* relief from pain
* confidence when smiling
* less fear
* trust
* safety
* predictability
* a better appearance
* a solution they have delayed for months or years

A furniture company thinks it sells custom furniture.

The customer buys:

* a home that feels finished
* storage that finally makes sense
* a kitchen that fits the actual space
* a premium look
* less chaos
* better daily comfort

A renovation company thinks it sells renovation work.

The customer buys:

* less stress
* a finished apartment
* fewer mistakes
* someone who handles the process
* predictable timelines
* trust that the job will not become a nightmare

## Research Inputs

When available, analyze the following:

* business name
* website
* niche
* city
* product or service
* target customer
* price level
* current offer
* current headline
* current CTA
* current homepage
* testimonials
* before/after examples
* social media
* Google reviews
* competitors
* desired action
* tone preference

If only partial information is available, still produce useful copy, but keep it safe and grounded.

## Research Questions

Before writing, answer these internally:

### 1. What does the business sell?

Do not stop at the category.

Weak:
"Design interior."

Better:
"Design interior for homeowners who want a beautiful home but do not know how to combine layout, colors, furniture, lighting, and budget."

### 2. Who is the buyer?

Identify the most likely buyer.

For local businesses, the buyer is often:

* a homeowner
* a parent
* a bride or groom
* a business owner
* a manager
* a patient
* a person with an urgent problem
* someone comparing multiple providers

### 3. What is the buyer’s real pain?

Separate surface pain from deep pain.

Example:

Surface pain:
"I need a dentist."

Deep pain:
"I am embarrassed by my smile and afraid the treatment will hurt or cost too much."

Surface pain:
"I need furniture."

Deep pain:
"My apartment feels unfinished, cluttered, and not like the home I imagined."

Surface pain:
"I need a website."

Deep pain:
"My business looks less serious than it actually is, and I may be losing clients because of that."

### 4. What result does the buyer want?

The result is not always the technical deliverable.

A landing page is not the result.

More qualified leads are the result.

A renovation is not the result.

A home that feels complete and stress-free is the result.

A dental implant is not the result.

Being able to smile, eat, and speak confidently is the result.

### 5. What are the buyer’s objections?

Typical objections:

* Is this too expensive?
* Can I trust them?
* Will this take too long?
* What if the result is not good?
* Are they better than the alternatives?
* Will they understand what I want?
* Will they pressure me?
* Is this worth it now?
* Can I postpone this?
* Have they done this before?

### 6. What proof is available?

Look for:

* testimonials
* reviews
* case studies
* portfolio
* before/after images
* years of experience
* certifications
* process
* guarantees
* media appearances
* client logos
* project examples

If proof is missing, the copy must compensate with clarity, process, and lower-friction CTAs.

### 7. What is the trust gap?

The trust gap is the missing reason someone does not act.

Examples:

* No clear CTA
* No proof
* No pricing context
* No process explanation
* No human face
* No specific benefits
* Too much technical language
* No examples
* Weak headline
* Generic service descriptions

The copy must reduce the trust gap.

### 8. What is the conversion goal?

Every output must have one primary action.

Examples:

* Book a consultation
* Request an offer
* Send a WhatsApp message
* Schedule an appointment
* Download a guide
* Ask for pricing
* Fill out a form
* Reply to the message

Do not mix too many goals.

## Business Diagnosis

After research, create a short business diagnosis.

Use this format internally:

Business:
Niche:
Likely buyer:
Main buyer pain:
Desired outcome:
Main objection:
Trust gap:
Best angle:
Primary CTA:

Example:

Business:
Local dental clinic

Niche:
Dentistry / dental implants

Likely buyer:
Adults who delayed treatment because of fear, cost, or lack of trust

Main buyer pain:
They want to fix their smile but are afraid of pain, price, or choosing the wrong clinic

Desired outcome:
A safe, clear, confident path to treatment

Main objection:
"I need to trust the doctor before I book"

Trust gap:
The website explains services, but does not reduce fear enough

Best angle:
Clear consultation, calm explanation, no pressure

Primary CTA:
Book an initial consultation

## Market Awareness

Identify how aware the buyer is.

### Level 1 — Unaware

The buyer does not know they have a problem.

Use educational copy.

Example:
"De ce multe site-uri pierd clienți în primele 5 secunde."

### Level 2 — Problem Aware

The buyer knows the problem but not the solution.

Use problem-focused copy.

Example:
"Primești trafic, dar puține cereri de ofertă?"

### Level 3 — Solution Aware

The buyer knows the type of solution.

Use differentiation.

Example:
"Landing page creată pentru cereri de ofertă, nu doar pentru design."

### Level 4 — Product Aware

The buyer knows your business.

Use proof and offer.

Example:
"Vezi cum ar putea arăta prima secțiune a site-ului tău rescrisă pentru conversii."

### Level 5 — Most Aware

The buyer is ready to act.

Use direct CTA.

Example:
"Programează o analiză gratuită a paginii tale."

## Customer Language

Use the language the customer would use.

Avoid overly technical words unless the buyer expects them.

For local service businesses, simple language usually converts better.

Bad:
"Optimizăm arhitectura informațională pentru eficientizarea parcursului utilizatorului."

Better:
"Facem pagina mai clară, astfel încât oamenii să înțeleagă rapid ce oferi și cum pot cere o ofertă."

## Competitor Awareness

When competitors are known, identify:

* what they all say
* what phrases they repeat
* what claims are generic
* what angle is missing
* what the business can own

Do not copy competitors.

Use competitors to find white space.

Example:

If all furniture companies say:
"Mobilă la comandă de calitate"

Find a more specific angle:
"Mobilier gândit pentru spații reale, unde fiecare centimetru contează."

## Offer Clarity

A strong offer answers:

* What do I get?
* Who is it for?
* What problem does it solve?
* Why should I trust it?
* What happens next?
* How do I start?

If these are unclear, the copy must clarify them.

## Proof Mapping

Map proof to buyer doubt.

Buyer doubt:
"Will this work for me?"

Proof:
Before/after examples, case studies, testimonials.

Buyer doubt:
"Can I trust them?"

Proof:
Reviews, team photos, process, certifications.

Buyer doubt:
"Will this be expensive?"

Proof:
Pricing context, consultation, transparent estimate.

Buyer doubt:
"Will this be stressful?"

Proof:
Clear process, project manager, timeline explanation.

## Opening Line Creation

The opening line must feel personal.

It should be based on something real.

Good opening lines:

"Am intrat pe site-ul vostru și mi-a plăcut că puneți accent pe mobilier făcut exact pe spațiul clientului. Totuși, cred că mesajul principal poate fi înțeles mai rapid."

"Am văzut că aveți mai multe servicii pentru renovări, dar pe prima secțiune nu este imediat clar care este motivul principal pentru care cineva ar trebui să vă aleagă."

"Mi-a plăcut portofoliul vostru, dar cred că homepage-ul poate transforma mai multe vizite în cereri de ofertă dacă pune mai repede în față beneficiul principal."

Bad opening lines:

"Am văzut site-ul și este foarte frumos."

"Cred că aveți nevoie de marketing."

"Vă pot ajuta să creșteți vânzările."

"Am o ofertă pentru dumneavoastră."

## Research Quality Checklist

Before writing, confirm:

* Do I know what the business sells?
* Do I know who the buyer is?
* Do I know what the buyer wants?
* Do I know what the buyer fears?
* Do I know what action the copy should drive?
* Do I know what trust gap must be reduced?
* Do I have at least one specific angle?

If not, do a lighter output and avoid strong claims.

## Final Research Rule

Research is not about collecting information.

Research is about finding the reason the buyer would care.

If you cannot explain why the buyer should care, you are not ready to write.
